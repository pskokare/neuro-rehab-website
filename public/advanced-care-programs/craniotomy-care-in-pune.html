Understanding Recovery After a Craniotomy

A craniotomy is a surgical procedure where a section of the skull is temporarily removed to allow a neurosurgeon to access the brain. It is performed for a variety of critical reasons, including:

  • Removing a brain tumor
  • Clipping a brain aneurysm
  • Draining a blood clot or hematoma (often after a TBI)
  • Relieving intracranial pressure

Brain tumors, a common reason for a craniotomy, are a significant health concern, with over 28,000 cases registered in India annually. Regardless of the reason for the surgery, the brain has undergone a major event and needs active, guided support to heal.

Specialized rehabilitation is essential because of neuroplasticity. Your brain has an incredible capacity to rewire itself, and our intensive therapy programs provide the structured stimulation needed to guide this process, helping healthy areas of the brain learn to take over lost functions.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

How long will my recovery take after a craniotomy?

Recovery from brain surgery is a marathon, not a sprint. The timeline is highly individual and depends on the reason for the surgery, the area of the brain affected, and your overall health. While initial healing takes weeks, a comprehensive rehabilitation program to maximize functional recovery can take several months.

I feel so tired all the time since my surgery. Is this normal?

Yes, profound fatigue is one of the most common and persistent symptoms after a craniotomy. Your brain is using an immense amount of energy to heal. Our rehabilitation program is specifically designed to manage this fatigue. We teach you energy conservation techniques and use a graded approach to therapy to help you gradually rebuild your stamina without over-exertion.

Will the cognitive problems I'm experiencing, like memory loss, get better?

For many people, cognitive function improves significantly with time and specialized rehabilitation. Our cognitive therapy program is designed to stimulate your brain and help it form new connections (neuroplasticity). We also teach you powerful compensatory strategies to help you manage any lingering challenges effectively.

How soon after my surgery should I start rehabilitation?

You should begin a comprehensive inpatient rehabilitation program as soon as your neurosurgeon determines you are medically stable and clears you to be discharged from the acute care hospital. Starting intensive, specialized rehab early is crucial for achieving the best possible long-term outcome.
