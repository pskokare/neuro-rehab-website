What is a Stroke?

A stroke is a "brain attack" that occurs when the blood supply to a part of the brain is cut off, depriving brain cells of oxygen and causing them to die. This damage can lead to lasting disability. In India, stroke is a leading cause of disability, with studies showing that roughly 70% of survivors require ongoing assistance with daily activities. This makes specialized rehabilitation an absolute necessity.

There are two main types of stroke:

  • Ischemic Stroke: This is the most common type, caused by a blood clot that blocks an artery in the brain.
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: This occurs when a weakened blood vessel in the brain ruptures and bleeds into the surrounding tissue.

Recognizing the signs and acting F.A.S.T. is critical:

  • F - Face Drooping: Is one side of the face numb or drooping?
  • A - Arm Weakness: Is one arm weak or unable to be lifted?
  • S - Speech Difficulty: Is speech slurred or hard to understand?
  • T - Time to Call for Help: Immediate medical attention is crucial

Introduction to Stroke Rehabilitation

Stroke rehabilitation is a specialized, proactive program designed to help you relearn skills lost after brain damage. It is built on the principle of neuroplasticity—the brain's amazing ability to rewire itself. Through intensive, repetitive, and task-specific therapy, we can encourage healthy parts of the brain to take over the functions of the damaged areas.

The goal is to maximize your functional independence, improve your quality of life, and help you return to your community. The first 3-6 months after a stroke are often called the "Golden Period" because the brain is most capable of rapid change, making early and intensive rehabilitation essential.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

My father can't move his left side at all. Can he recover?

This condition is called hemiplegia. Recovery is possible. Through intensive therapy that stimulates neuroplasticity, the brain can often find new ways to send signals to the affected limbs. The extent of recovery varies, but our goal is always to maximize movement and function to the greatest degree possible.

What causes swallowing problems (dysphagia) after a stroke?

Swallowing is a complex process controlled by nerves and muscles. A stroke can damage the part of the brain that coordinates this process, leading to weakness or discoordination of the swallowing muscles. This is a very common issue that our speech Physiotherapists are experts at treating.

How can our family help in the recovery process?

Family support is crucial. Your role includes providing encouragement, celebrating small successes, and, most importantly, participating in the training we provide. We will teach you how to assist with exercises safely and communicate effectively, making you a vital member of the rehabilitation team.

He understands me but struggles to find the right words. What can be done?

This sounds like Expressive Aphasia, one of the most common communication disorders after a stroke. It is not a loss of intelligence, but a problem with language retrieval. Our speech Physiotherapists use specific, targeted exercises to help the brain build new pathways and improve word-finding ability.

What is the "recovery plateau" I've heard about?

A plateau is a period where recovery seems to slow down after the initial rapid gains of the "Golden Period." It does not mean recovery has stopped. It often means a new, more challenging, or different therapeutic approach is needed to continue stimulating the brain. We are experts at designing programs to help patients push through plateaus.
