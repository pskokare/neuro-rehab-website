Understanding Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

A spinal cord injury involves damage to the spinal cord itself—the vital communication highway that carries signals between your brain and the rest of your body. This disruption affects movement, sensation, and other body functions below the level of the injury.

The causes of SCI are often traumatic, with studies indicating that road traffic accidents and falls account for over 75% of spinal injuries in India. Understanding the specifics of your injury is the first step in creating a realistic and effective rehabilitation plan.

Complete vs. Incomplete Injury
  • Complete SCI: A total loss of sensory and motor function below the level of injury. The focus of rehab is on adaptation and learning new ways to achieve independence.
  • Incomplete SCI: Some function or sensation remains below the level of injury. In these cases, rehab also focuses on harnessing the potential for further neurological recovery.
Paraplegia and Tetraplegia (Quadriplegia)
  • Paraplegia: Affects the trunk, legs, and pelvic organs. Arm and hand function is typically not affected.
  • Tetraplegia (Quadriplegia): Affects all four limbs—both arms and both legs—as well as the trunk.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Will I ever walk again after my spinal cord injury?

This is the most important question for many. The potential for walking depends entirely on whether your injury is "complete" or "incomplete." For a complete injury, the focus is on maximizing independence using a wheelchair. For some incomplete injuries, intensive rehabilitation can help regain some walking function. We will always provide you with an honest, realistic assessment of your potential and set goals that are meaningful for you.

What is a pressure sore and why is it so dangerous?

A pressure sore is a skin wound caused by prolonged pressure that cuts off circulation. For individuals with SCI, who may not be able to feel these areas, they can become very deep and dangerous, leading to severe infections. Preventing them through regular pressure relief and skin checks is one of the most critical skills we teach.

What is Autonomic Dysreflexia (AD)?

AD is a medical emergency that can affect those with injuries at the T6 level or higher. It is an over-reaction of the nervous system to a stimulus below the injury (like a full bladder), causing a dangerous spike in blood pressure. We provide critical education on how to recognize and react to the symptoms immediately.

Can I learn to drive or go back to work after my SCI?

For many people with SCI, the answer is yes! Our Occupational Physiotherapists are experts in this area. They can provide guidance on vehicle hand controls and adaptive driving, as well as work with you on vocational rehabilitation to find the tools and strategies needed to return to a fulfilling career.
